About Organ Transplant Technology

Understanding the science and process of organ transplantation

What is an organ transplant?

An organ transplant is when a donor gives their healthy organ to somebody who has an organ beyond healing. Surgeons will remove the organ from the healthy donor and transplant it into the person. Some examples of organs that are commonly transplanted include the liver, kidney, heart, lungs, and more.

The Process of Organ Transplantation

The first step in ensuring an organ transplant goes to plan is making sure that the person receiving the organ has an eligible donor. All this means is ensuring the person donating their organ matches a certain criteria. Usually, the doctor has to evaluate the donor and recipient on a case-by-case basis. Commonly, these are some of the things they look for when transplanting: healthy organ, organ size, blood type, and type of organ to prevent the recipient's body from rejecting the new organ. Next, they prioritize the patient's condition, and based on their condition, they will receive the organ first. The final part is the actual transplant, where the donor's organ is placed into the patient.

How Does it Work?

Overall, this technology works by removing a damaged organ from a patient's body and removing the organ from the recipient while keeping it alive and transplanting it into the recipient to keep that part working and save a life.

What Is to be expected?

More on Why.

An organ transplant involves removing an organ from a person's body and putting it into someone who is ill or dying. It can save the life of the person who receives the organ. In 2022, over 40000 organs were donated in the USA alone, which means around 41000 people were able to have a second chance at life or increase their lifespan (Cindyyoung, 2023b). The donor has two parts of the organ or is dead, in which they have consented to use their organs for the purpose of transplantation. This is why it is being used to this day.
